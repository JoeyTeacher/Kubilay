Expressions We Practiced & Learned

"What have you been up to?"

A very common way to ask 'What have you done recently?'

  • "Hey, long time no see! What have you been up to?"

"Once or twice a month/week"

Great for talking about habits.

  • "I go to the gym once or twice a week."
  • "I've only been to that restaurant once or twice."
  • "I smoke once or twice a week."

"I smoke about a pack a day"

Use about to mean approximately.

  • "My grandfather used to smoke about a pack a day."
  • "I go to the cinema about twice a month."

"You must love..."

To make a guess about someone's feelings.

  • "You spend so much time in the garden, you must love gardening!"

"When do you want to meet?" / "When shall we meet?"

Useful for making plans.

  • "I'm free on Tuesday. When do you want to meet?"

"The doctor told us"

Use 'told us' instead of 'said to us' when delivering information or instructions.

  • "The doctor told us not to worry."
  • "My girlfriend told me to wait for her outside the pub."
  • "My boss told me to get my reports in by Friday."

Natural English & Fluency Tips

Everyday Reductions

In natural, fast English, words often get reduced. For example:

"What have you been up to?"

Often sounds like: "Whad've ya bin up to?"

Emphasizing with Sound

Sometimes, we stretch out a word's sound to add emphasis, especially with numbers or duration.

Example with "about":

"I've been living in London aboooutt 6 years."

(Imagine drawing out the "ou" sound with your hand to show the duration/approximation.)

'TH' Sounds (Voiced & Unvoiced)

We also focused on the 'TH' sounds. It's important to remember that the 'TH' sound can be either:

  • Voiced: Your vocal cords vibrate when you make the sound (e.g., put your hand on your throat when you say it, you'll feel a vibration).
  • Unvoiced: Your vocal cords do not vibrate; it's just air passing through.

Voiced 'TH' Words:

  • this
  • that
  • these
  • those
  • they

Unvoiced 'TH' Words:

  • think
  • thank
  • through
  • thing
  • three
