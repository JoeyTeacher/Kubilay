Language Highlights: Your New Tools! üõ†Ô∏è

Here are some key language points you used and learned today. Read through these examples!

First Conditional (Likely Outcome)

We use this for situations that are real or very likely to happen. The value of this is clearly communicating your future plans or warnings.

  • "If I improve my English, I will get a promotion."
  • "If my boss doesn't give me a raise in 3 months, I'll quit."
  • "When I next go to McDonald's, I will buy a double cheeseburger." (Using when makes the situation even more certain!)
  • "If I can, I will."

Second Conditional (Imaginary Outcome)

We use this for imagined, unlikely, or impossible situations. The value of this structure is expressing wishes, dreams, and hypothetical advice.

  • "If I had a week off work next week, I would fly to Turkey for a quick trip."
  • "If I were a superhero, I would fly."
  • "If I missed my bus, I'd have to take a taxi." (Pam's unlikely scenario)
  • "If I could, I would."

Can vs. Be Able To (Ability)

You mastered the important difference! Using be able to is key when discussing external obstacles preventing ability (which can does not usually express).

  • "Can you play soccer?" (Internal skill: Do you know how?)
  • "I can speak English, but right now I am unable to (because the dentist injected my mouth)." (Temporary external problem.)
  • "I wouldn't be able to save money (because the high London rent is the external problem)."

New Vocabulary & Prefixes

  • Tardy: A formal word for always being late.
  • Punctual: Always being on time.
  • Sleep in: To sleep later than usual (especially on the weekend).
  • Un- (prefix): Means not or the opposite action (e.g., unsafe, unfair, unlock, unwell, uncomfortable). This prefix is very common and a powerful tool!
  • Talk shit about someone: (Informal) To speak negatively about someone when they are not present.
  • Grandfather Law (Expression): A rule that says a new law will not apply to people who were already doing that thing before the law changed.
