Habits & Routines: What's the Difference? ü§î

In class, we talked a lot about the word "habit." A habit is something you do frequently or regularly, almost automatically. Habits can be good (e.g., "I brush my teeth every morning") or bad (e.g., "I bite my nails when I'm nervous").

A routine is a series of habits or actions you do in a particular order. For example, "My morning routine is to wake up, drink coffee, and read the news."

Both habits and routines are often described using the Zero Conditional because they are things that are generally or always true for you. For example, "When I come home from work, I take off my clothes" is a routine for you, so we use a zero conditional sentence to describe it.

Conditional Sentences: Common Uses üìù

This section gives you a quick review of the most common ways we use the zero and first conditional. This will help you remember when to use each one!

Zero Conditional: General Truths

We use the zero conditional to talk about things that are always true. The sentence structure is: If/When + Present Simple, Present Simple.

  • Facts: Scientific facts, universal truths, and things that always happen under certain conditions.
    Example: "If you heat water to 100¬∞C, it boils."
  • Habits & Routines: Things that you do regularly or a frequent outcome of an action. Habits can be good, bad, or neutral.
    Example: "When I get home from work, I take off my shoes."
  • Rules & Instructions: Rules or instructions that are always followed.
    Example: "If a customer has a complaint, the manager handles it."

First Conditional: Future Possibilities

We use the first conditional to talk about a possible future situation and its probable result. The sentence structure is: If + Present Simple, will + Verb.

  • Predictions: Things you think will happen in the future based on a condition.
    Example: "If it rains tomorrow, the football game will be canceled."
  • Superstitions: Beliefs about good or bad luck related to a future event.
    Example: "If you walk under a ladder, you will have bad luck."
  • Plans & Promises: Things you plan to do or promise to do if a condition is met.
    Example: "If I finish my work early, I will go to the gym."
  • Offers & Advice: Giving someone a suggestion or an offer.
    Example: "If you want to lose weight, you will need to exercise."
  • Warnings: A sentence to warn someone about a definite negative future outcome.
    Example: "If you are late to work tomorrow, the boss will be unhappy."
  • Ability: To talk about an ability in a conditional situation.
    Example: "If you have an allergy, you can change the ingredients."

Modals of Certainty and Obligation

We also reviewed how to use modal verbs to talk about the certainty of a future outcome or a sense of obligation. You learned these important distinctions:

  • will: (100% certain) "The sun will come up tomorrow."
  • won't: (0% certain) "It won't snow tomorrow."
  • probably: (75% certain) "It probably won't rain."
  • might: (50% certain) "It might rain later."
  • must: (no choice/obligation) "I must approve the ready payments."

Grammar Review: Conditional Sentences üìù

Here's a quick review of how to form conditional sentences. This is important for accuracy!

Zero Conditional

Used for things that are always true, such as facts and habits.

Condition (If/When + Present Simple) Result (Present Simple)
If you heat water to 100 degrees, it boils.
When the sun goes down, it gets dark.

First Conditional

Used for a possible future situation and its definite result.

Condition (If + Present Simple) Result (will + Verb)
If it rains tomorrow, the football game will be canceled.
If she doesn't call me today, I'll be annoyed.
